# PigeonYang Cover Design Standard: 何同学

## Scope
Use this standard when the user's topic can truthfully become a concrete proof
frame: product, device, interface, prototype, experiment, physical action,
visual artifact, process scene, personal artifact, measurable result, price,
count, or other visible evidence.

Best-fit topics:

- product reviews where a device detail, price, history, flaw, feature, or named
  object creates curiosity
- prototypes, tools, rigs, machines, desks, keyboards, physical workflows, apps,
  or visible interfaces
- real tests with phones, screens, measurements, prices, quantities, distances,
  people counts, behavior data, taste tests, or comparison states
- everyday problems solved through strange overbuilt actions or services
- craft, material, science, camera, or visual experiments where the artifact or
  effect is inspectable
- personal/process topics when there is a concrete scene or artifact: workspace,
  letter, interview, room, production timeline, graduation, milestone, or
  behind-the-scenes apparatus

Do not use this standard for abstract essays, broad methodology, conceptual
systems, pure opinions, pure AI workflow explanations, or article-first
knowledge covers unless the user can provide real evidence: object, interface,
result, process artifact, human test, data screen, or concrete personal scene.
Do not invent a fake concept machine just to make an abstract topic look
tangible.

Do not copy 何同学's likeness, channel identity, logos, exact thumbnails, or
private studio assets. Use the user's own portrait, product, interface, process
scene, proof object, or a neutral subject.

## Core Design DNA
1. The shared engine is evidence-first curiosity, not one layout.
2. A high-performing cover shows a concrete proof unit or concrete scene before
   it explains a concept.
3. The proof unit can be an object, product, screen, machine, action, visual
   artifact, behavior result, people-count experiment, price, process scene, or
   personal artifact.
4. The subject supports the proof as operator, holder, maker, scale reference,
   tester, witness, rider, presenter, interview subject, or autobiographical
   anchor. The subject alone is rarely enough.
5. Text is usually proof-bound: screen UI, product name, price, number, binary
   label, object label, function phrase, or scene-native words.
6. The viewer question is concrete: what is this, how did they build/test it,
   what result did it show, why is this ordinary thing solved so strangely, or
   why is this personal/process scene worth watching?
7. If the cover needs a detached sentence to explain why it matters, the proof
   unit or selected paradigm is not strong enough.

## Cover Generation Engine
- Engine type: `Evidence-First Curiosity Engine` with `Product Curiosity`,
  `Build Proof`, `Measured Experiment`, `Absurd Utility`, `Visual Spectacle`,
  and `Personal Process Proof` branches.
- Viewer decision compressed: "There is something concrete here: a thing, test,
  build, result, scene, or personal/process proof. What is it, how did it
  happen, and why is it worth inspecting?"
- Topic-to-cover mechanism: choose the proof branch and internal paradigm first,
  then choose the sharpest frame: macro state, product reveal, built object,
  measured test, scale result, absurd action, visual artifact, or concrete
  process scene.
- Subject role: operator, holder, maker, tester, rider, witness, presenter,
  interviewer/interviewee, process guide, or autobiographical anchor.
- Pre-visual decision: decide what real proof can be shown honestly. If the
  answer is only "a metaphorical machine", "a workflow diagram", or "a clever
  headline", reject this engine.
- Drift risk: fake concept machines, generic AI dashboards, architecture
  diagrams, SaaS launch posters, text-first authority covers, product ads with
  no curiosity anchor, personal portraits with no process proof, or MrBeast
  stakes without truthful scale.

## Popular Paradigms
### Paradigm 1: Macro / Screen Proof State
- Evidence count: 6/93 primary assignments.
- Representative samples: 1, 16, 32, 38, 84, 87.
- Best-fit topics: invisible tech, screen states, behavior data, phone UI,
  small device details, practical tutorial proof, app result, tiny object
  controls, and measurement screens.
- Click promise: one close detail makes the invisible story visible.
- Topic translation: convert the topic into a single readable state on a real
  screen, object, button, UI, chart, or document.
- One-frame story: the proof detail is pushed close enough for the viewer to
  judge immediately.
- First read: screen, object detail, key, number, UI state, alert, chart, or
  measured value.
- Second read: hand, device edge, face blur, or scale cue.
- Text behavior: native screen/object text, one attached label, or no extra text.
- Composition: macro close-up; subject optional and secondary.
- Failure mode: a detached headline explains a detail that is not visible.
- Prompt contract: choose the real proof detail before adding title, face, or
  background.

### Paradigm 2: Prototype / Built Object Hero

  assembly lines, machines, sketches, keyboards, desks, fake platforms, and
  hand-built artifacts.
- Click promise: they actually built this thing.
- Topic translation: convert the topic into one working object, internal reveal,

- First read: prototype, machine, internal structure, large build, or interface

- Composition: object dominates; subject validates scale or authorship.
- Failure mode: balanced workbench with many small parts and no decisive object.
- Prompt contract: make the built object large enough to prove the claim.

### Paradigm 4: Measured / Scale Test
- Evidence count: 10/93 primary assignments.
- Representative samples: 6, 8, 13, 15, 21, 34, 42, 51, 54, 64.
- Best-fit topics: fast/slow, far/near, real/fake, old/new, expensive/cheap,

- Click promise: the visible test or scale decides the claim.
- Topic translation: convert the topic into two states, a count, a distance,
  price range, participant sample, repeated object field, or measurable result.
- One-frame story: subject holds, frames, triggers, or stands inside a test that
  can be judged in one glance.
- First read: count, price, result, two states, repeated test units, or scale
  setup.
- Second read: subject as organizer, tester, witness, or participant.
- Text behavior: one short label per state, a count label, price tag, or native
  result display.
- Composition: two clear states, repeated objects, participant/test scale, or
  an action frame that reveals the measurement.
- Failure mode: abstract before/after with no real proof objects or fake crowd.
- Prompt contract: use this only when the result can be shown as evidence.

### Paradigm 5: Absurd Utility Action

- Representative samples: 4, 11, 35, 36, 41, 46, 52, 74.
- Best-fit topics: everyday problems solved by strange overbuilt products,
  utility devices, playful services, functional gags, physical interventions,

- Topic translation: turn the practical benefit into a real action already

- First read: action, strange object, or utility setup.
- Second read: subject role and result cue.
- Text behavior: optional short function label if the action is not self-evident.
- Composition: action frame, not static product render.
- Failure mode: beauty shot of the invention with no visible use.
- Prompt contract: show the object performing its function.

### Paradigm 6: Craft / Visual Spectacle

- Topic translation: convert the topic into a visible material effect, document,

- One-frame story: the made effect is visible at its most surprising moment.
- First read: visual effect, material artifact, performance object, or camera
  proof.
- Second read: subject, hand, instrument, or scale cue proving it was made.
- Text behavior: native document text, object label, or no text.
- Composition: effect/artifact dominates; explanation stays out of frame.
- Failure mode: abstract art poster with no proof of making.
- Prompt contract: make the artifact/effect inspectable, not decorative.

### Paradigm 7: Personal / Process Documentary

- Representative samples: 14, 18, 19, 20, 31, 48, 53, 55, 58, 66, 73, 75, 81,

- Best-fit topics: creator milestones, reflective essays, behind-the-scenes
  process, interviews, workspace tours, school/work identity, production
  timelines, personal letters, room/desk identity, and learning/process stories.
- Click promise: this personal/process claim is anchored in a concrete scene or
  artifact.
- Topic translation: convert the personal point into a scene, room, workspace,
  letter, interview frame, production apparatus, graduation/milestone object, or
  behind-the-scenes proof.
- One-frame story: the subject is inside a real scene that proves the process or
  life moment.
- First read: face/scene pair, process artifact, workspace, interview frame,
  letter, room, or milestone object.
- Second read: title clue, prop, background context, or person-to-person
  relationship.
- Text behavior: restrained; use scene-native signs, one large simple phrase, or
  no text.
- Composition: concrete scene first, portrait second. The face must not float
  alone as a generic authority portrait.
- Failure mode: generic portrait plus motivational headline.
- Prompt contract: show the process/personal artifact that makes the claim
  real.

## Topic Translation Rules
- First choose the internal paradigm and proof unit.
- Abstract topics can use this engine only if grounded in a real artifact:
  working interface, generated output, physical prototype, data screen, before/
  after comparison, document pile, process scene, personal artifact, human
  experiment, or visible test.
- Concrete topics should become closer, larger, stranger, more judgeable, more
  product-specific, or more action-driven.
- Required translation variables: selected internal paradigm, proof unit or
  process scene, why it is surprising, subject role/action, one-second viewer
  question, proof-bound text if needed, and forbidden drift.
- Personal/process topics must answer: what scene or artifact proves this is not
  just a portrait?
- Forbidden translation moves: invented concept machines, floating icons,
  holograms, AI robots, dashboard collages, PPT diagrams, product-ad beauty
  shots without curiosity, and large detached thesis titles.
- Example translation: "AI content flywheel" is rejected unless there is a real
  interface, printed output, physical workflow artifact, measured result, human
  test, or process scene to show.

## Cover Storyboard Rules
- Story moment: freeze the instant of proof: the button is pressed, the screen
  shows the result, the product is held up, the machine is opened, two objects
  are compared, the strange invention is being used, the visual effect becomes
  inspectable, or the process scene reveals the personal claim.
- Visible conflict: invisible vs visible, real vs fake, fast vs slow, hidden
  internals vs polished product, absurd solution vs ordinary problem, expensive
  number vs object, large sample vs individual opinion, made effect vs ordinary
  scene, personal claim vs concrete process artifact.
- Subject task/action: hold, point, operate, ride, taste, compare, demonstrate,
  stand inside a scene, present a screen, witness the result, or participate in
  the process moment.
- Proof object: device state, product body, prototype, UI, price, comparison
  object, artifact, repeated test objects, participant count, document, sketch,
  internal mechanism, action result, room/workspace, letter, or interview scene.
- Viewer question: "What exactly am I seeing, and why did they do this?"
- Forbidden static compositions: central portrait with unsupported title,
  abstract workflow diagram, decorative prototype, UI-card collage, clean
  metaphor object, or a machine that exists only as symbolism.
- Example storyboard: a real screen/result/object/test/process scene is pushed
  near the camera; the subject is beside or inside it as operator or witness,
  with one short proof-bound label if needed.

## Design Layout Brief Rules
- First read: selected proof unit, product, screen state, comparison, price,
  action, artifact, process scene, personal artifact, or human-scale test.
- Second read: subject's action or face confirming use, scale, authorship,
  emotion, or result.
- Third read: clue such as number, label, internal mechanism, cable, reflection,
  hand, repeated object, participant count, prop, date, room detail, or object
  label.
- Layout zones: one dominant proof/scene zone; subject behind, beside, above,
  inside, or partially obscured by proof; optional labels bound to proof.
- Visual weight: proof/scene 55-85%; subject 10-40%; environment only as
  context.
- Reading path: proof first, action/face second, clue third.
- Negative space: black studio void, clean wall, screen area, product surface,
  blurred background, room plane, or object-native text zone.
- Forbidden layouts: symmetrical article-header diagram, many input/output
  cards, three-column process explanation, decorative flywheel, detached title
  block, and "productivity system" collage.

## Copy Hierarchy Rules
- Main title: usually none. If needed, use one proof-bound phrase, number, price,
  product name, binary label, count, function word, or scene-native phrase.
- Subtitle: avoid.
- State labels: use only for visible comparison states or test scale, such as
  `快 / 慢`, `真的 / 假的`, `500人`, `1000人`, `自动开灯`, `5G`, `¥49999`, or a
  product name.
- Object/zone binding: text must sit on the screen, product, label plate, scene
  sign, or directly above the corresponding object/side.
- Isolation rules: labels must be visually fused to their proof through
  distance, color, plate, screen placement, perspective, or repeated-object
  grouping.
- Forbidden adjacency: never combine a detached main title with object labels so
  the viewer reads a confusing sentence.
- Removal rule: if text explains a concept rather than naming visible proof,
  remove it or route away from this engine.

## Layout Patterns
### Pattern 1: Macro / Screen Proof State
- Use when: one screen, button, key, UI metric, price, document, or product
  detail proves the hook.
- Composition: extreme close-up; hand/device edge visible; subject optional and
  secondary.
- Text: native screen/object text or one attached label.
- Avoid: explaining the detail with a large floating title.

### Pattern 2: Product / Prototype Hero
- Use when: a product, prototype, rig, or artifact has a visually strange form,
  price, internal structure, or named identity.
- Composition: proof object fills most of frame; subject behind or beside it as
  operator/witness/scale.
- Text: product name, price, function word, or no text.
- Avoid: generic product ad without a curiosity anchor.

### Pattern 3: Measured / Scale Test
- Use when: the story is real/fake, fast/slow, old/new, charged/dead, expensive/
  cheap, far/near, participant count, or measured result.
- Composition: subject holds two objects/screens, frame splits around two real
  states, or repeated objects show scale.
- Text: one short label per object/side, count, or price.
- Avoid: abstract before/after diagrams with no objects.

### Pattern 4: Absurd Utility Action
- Use when: the object solves an everyday problem in a playful or overbuilt way.
- Composition: action is happening: riding, launching, drinking, opening,
  tasting, testing, pressing, holding, aiming, erasing, or operating.
- Text: optional short function label if the action is not self-evident.
- Avoid: static beauty shot of the invention.

### Pattern 5: Craft / Visual Spectacle
- Use when: a made artifact, material effect, animation trick, camera view, or
  visual illusion is the click object.
- Composition: effect/artifact dominates; subject or hand proves scale/making.
- Text: native document/object text or none.
- Avoid: abstract art poster with no proof of making.

### Pattern 6: Personal / Process Documentary
- Use when: a personal claim, creator-process story, interview, room, workspace,
  milestone, or behind-the-scenes process has a real scene or artifact.
- Composition: concrete scene/proof first; subject is inside the scene, not
  floating in front of a slogan.
- Text: one simple phrase, scene-native sign, or no text.
- Avoid: generic portrait plus reflective headline.

## Subject Rules
Preserve the user's identity from reference images, but redesign pose and
expression around the proof action or process scene. Good subject roles: holder,
operator, tester, rider, watcher, scale reference, organizer, witness, maker,
interview subject, or process guide. Good expressions: focused curiosity, calm
proof, slight mischievous smile, serious comparison, or quiet reflection when a
process artifact is visible. Avoid arms-crossed authority, default thinking
pose, detached presenter, and exaggerated shock without object evidence.

## Reference Image Handling
Reference images lock identity, face traits, hair, glasses, clothing cues,
product appearance, interface appearance, process artifacts, or brand assets.
They do not lock pose, crop, lighting, or background unless the user explicitly
says so. Convert static portraits into proof-related interaction or process
scenes. If the user only supplies a portrait and no real proof object, interface,
result, action, test, process scene, or personal artifact, ask for proof or route
to another engine.

## Identity And Final Prompt Firewall
- Internal routing may name 何同学, but the final GPT Image 2 prompt must not
  contain `何同学`, `He Tongxue`, `何同学-inspired`, `He Tongxue-inspired`, `in
  the style of 何同学`, `like 何同学`, or equivalent creator-name shortcuts.
- Express the pattern through concrete mechanics: evidence-first curiosity,
  selected internal paradigm, dominant proof unit or process scene,
  subject-as-operator/witness, proof-bound labels, real product/studio lighting,
  and specific interaction.
- If a portrait reference is supplied, identity preservation outranks all
  creator-pattern rules. The subject must preserve the user's identity traits and
  must not resemble any public creator.
- Add a final negative constraint that the generated subject must not resemble
  any public creator, without naming 何同学.

## Text Rules
Use text as proof, not explanation. Good text is visible screen state, product
name, price, count, short binary label, function word, object label, scene-native
word, or native document text. Keep Chinese labels short and bold. Avoid article
headlines, slogans, thesis claims, and multi-label process maps. If text could
be removed without weakening the real proof unit, remove it.

## Typography Layout System
Protected text zones are proof-bound: phone screens, device surfaces, giant
product-name overlays on the object, price labels on products, labels above two
compared objects, count labels attached to repeated objects, native document
text, or scene signage. Use bold, high-contrast, feed-readable type. Backing
shapes are allowed only when they behave like stickers, screens, label plates,
or product UI. When the frame is cluttered, either move text onto the proof unit
or remove it.

## Color And Lighting Rules
Use real-camera product, studio, or process-scene lighting. Common palettes:
black/white product contrast, phone-screen blue, red/blue test polarity, yellow
utility object, graphite machinery, transparent product glow, product-native
color, clean lab/studio light, or practical room/workspace light. Saturation can
be strong when it belongs to the object, UI, comparison state, or visual
spectacle. Avoid generic AI purple, cyberpunk glow, fictional holograms, and
soft editorial gradients.

## Hook Mechanics
- A real detail makes invisible technology visible.
- A physical object proves "we built this".
- A product property, price, flaw, or name creates curiosity.
- A comparison makes the result instantly judgeable.
- A strange action proves the invention's function.
- A made artifact or visual effect proves craft.
- A people-count or price-range test converts opinion into proof.
- A concrete process scene converts personal narrative into visible evidence.
- A screen state converts behavior or data into visible proof.

## GPT Image 2 Prompt Contract
Every prompt must include:
- target platform, aspect ratio, and exact pixel canvas
- selected internal paradigm
- the real proof object/interface/result/action/test/person-count/process scene
  to show
- subject role and physical interaction with the proof or scene
- camera distance and perspective: macro close-up, product hero, held-to-camera,
  overhead, oblique internal reveal, action frame, repeated-object test scale, or
  concrete process scene
- exact on-cover text, or "no text"
- where each text element is attached in the proof world
- realistic product/studio/process-scene lighting and material detail
- platform crop-safe zones
- explicit rejection of fake concept machines, abstract workflow diagrams,
  detached titles, generic AI dashboards, unsupported portraits, and public
  creator likeness

## Negative Constraints
No 何同学 likeness replication. No channel logo imitation. No invented concept
machine for abstract topics. No generic AI robot, hologram dashboard, floating
icons, process diagram, article-title poster, text-first authority cover, dense
architecture, or decorative workflow flywheel. No object so small that it only
acts as background decoration. No labels detached from their proof units. No
generic portrait for a personal/process topic unless a concrete scene or artifact
is visible. No public creator names in final prompts.

## User Intake Questions
1. 你要发哪个平台？横版、竖版还是都要？
2. 这个主题里真实可展示的物件、界面、数据、实验结果、动作、人群测试、过程场景或个人物件是什么？
3. 是否有产品图、界面图、样机图、截图、手稿、输出样例、过程照、实拍参考或个人参考照？
4. 这次更适合哪个内部范式：宏观证据、样机/产品主角、产品好奇、量化/规模测试、荒诞实用动作、手工视觉实验，还是个人/过程纪录？
5. 封面文字是否可以不用完整标题，只保留产品名、数字、价格、状态、场景词或功能标签？
6. 如果没有真实证明物或过程场景，是否改用更适合抽象观点/机制解释的 child skill？

## Quality Checklist
- A selected internal paradigm is named before style is chosen.
- A real proof unit or process scene is named before composition.
- The first read is an object, screen, product, comparison, price, action,
  artifact, data, personal/process scene, or human-scale test.
- The subject interacts with proof or acts as witness/scale/process anchor.
- Text is short, proof-bound, and not a detached thesis.
- The frame is not a balanced workflow diagram or concept workbench.
- Personal/process topics include a concrete artifact or scene.
- Product topics include a curiosity anchor, not only a beauty shot.
- The proof unit creates a one-second viewer question.
- Platform ratio and target canvas are explicit.
- Reference image preserves identity but not weak source pose.
- Final prompt avoids creator-name shortcuts and public creator likeness.
