## Design DNA
### Core
1. Authority-first talking head: the face is usually the proof object. The cover asks viewers to trust a hard claim because the host is looking directly at them.
2. One blunt intellectual claim: the cover text is not a title summary; it is a condensed thesis, warning, command, or contrarian reframe.
3. Minimal visual world: gray/black background, shallow depth of field, simple studio lighting, little to no decorative clutter.
4. Typography carries the story: large bold sans-serif text, white or black, often with a white highlight plate behind the decisive phrase.
5. Personal transformation and creator-economy topics are translated into identity-level statements: "you are wrong", "you can become", "the future belongs", "write like this".
6. Serious expression over spectacle: calm intensity, direct eye contact, slight hand gesture, or focused pose replaces exaggerated emotion.

### Conditional
- A blurred UI or feed background appears when the topic is platform growth or software/workflow.
- Red underline or pale yellow emphasis appears sparingly for an important word, but not as a colorful palette.
- Centered face is used when the text wraps around or above/below the subject; right-side face with left-side text is more common.
- Tactical videos can use numbers, prompts, or "steal these" wording, but still stay visually austere.

### Weak
- Older covers may use thinner, more cinematic text and microphone/talking-head composition. The recent recognizable system is the bolder text-left/face-right formula.
- Some public analysis discusses Dan's previous concept-visual thumbnails. The 2025-2026 sample set shows a cleaner talking-head authority system, so the child skill should prioritize the current system unless the user asks for older visual-explainer energy.

## Cover Generation Engine
Dan Koe uses a hybrid `Authority Engine` + `Transformation Engine` + `Utility Clarity Engine`.

The viewer decision compressed into the cover is: "Does this creator understand the hidden rule behind my stalled growth, thinking, work, or identity, and can he give me a clearer path?"

The cover promise is not spectacle. It is diagnosis. A raw topic becomes a clickable cover by turning it into a compressed judgment:
- your current belief is wrong
- the future belongs to a different type of person
- this behavior will change your life
- the skill/problem is simpler than you think
- a small system creates a large personal outcome

The subject role is `expert` and `witness`. The face says: "I have seen this pattern, and I am telling you the truth directly." The subject is rarely a prize, victim, or comic actor.

Before visual style is selected, the topic must become one sentence of authority: a hard claim, warning, command, paradox, or identity reframe. If that sentence is weak, the thumbnail fails no matter how good the lighting is.

## Topic Translation Rules
- Abstract topics are translated into a person-level belief, not a literal diagram. Example: "AI content flywheel" should become "your content system is backwards" or "your ideas need a system", not a glowing machine unless the topic genuinely needs a product proof.
- Concrete tools and workflows are translated into a simple gain or shortcut. Example: "AI workflow" becomes "steal these 6 prompts" or "the fastest way to grow".
- Personal development topics are translated into future identity or present mistake.
- Business topics are translated into market-shift claims, death/new-era claims, or one-person leverage claims.
- Creative topics are translated into paradoxical prescriptions: boredom, focus, writing, clarity, restraint.
- Required translation variables: viewer pain, wrong belief, stronger belief, one-sentence claim, whether the topic is authority/identity/utility, proof object if any, and how much Dan-style face authority is needed.
- Forbidden translation moves: giant machines, money piles, exaggerated facial shock, multi-object collages, saturated neon, busy comparison dashboards, cute metaphors, or complex infographics. Those drift into MrBeast, finance YouTube, SaaS demo, or generic AI-product cover style.

## Cover Storyboard Rules
- The one-frame story is a direct confrontation or quiet revelation: the expert looks at the viewer while the text names the belief they need to accept.
- Visible conflict is usually mental, not physical: wrong skill vs right skill, scattered interests vs generalist future, not writing vs life change, overstimulation vs boredom.
- The subject should actively communicate authority: direct stare, lean-in, finger-to-temple, hands together, mid-explanation gesture, or grounded front-facing posture.
- The proof object is usually the claim itself plus the subject's authority. Use a blurred UI, notes, document, or canvas only when it strengthens a workflow/software topic.
- The viewer should wonder: "Am I making this mistake?" or "What does he know that I don't?"
- Static poster failure: no generic floating symbols, no product dashboard as the main subject, no hero illustration, no decorative AI swirl, no ordinary portrait with a title pasted beside it.

## Design Layout Brief Rules
- First read: the compressed claim in huge type.
- Second read: the face/expression as proof and emotional tone.
- Third read: the emphasized word, number, underline, or subtle background proof.
- Layout zones: left 50-60% is the protected text zone; right 35-45% is the face/upper body zone. Center face is allowed only when text is split above/below or the frame is vertical.
- Visual weight: text and face are both dominant; any background object must stay secondary and blurred.
- Reading path: claim first, highlighted phrase second, face third, then optional background cue.
- Negative space: protect a dark or light smooth field behind text before generating typography. Do not place text over detailed UI unless a solid plate isolates it.
- Forbidden layouts: full dashboard screenshot, three-column comparison, many badges, before/after panels, icons scattered around the head, or text blocks close enough to form unintended phrases.

## Copy Hierarchy Rules
- Main title: one concise thesis, command, warning, or identity reframe. Usually 3-9 words; can be longer only when broken into 2-3 clean lines.
- Subtitle: usually absent. If present, it must be a quiet secondary phrase, not a competing headline.
- State labels: rarely needed. The Dan Koe pattern prefers one sentence over labeled zones.
- Object/zone binding: if a label appears, it must bind to a blurred UI, document, or prompt list, not float near the main title.
- Isolation rules: highlighted words may sit on white plates; separate lines must share one reading order and not accidentally connect with another label.
- Forbidden adjacency: do not place a short state label next to the main title if it can be read as the start of the sentence.
- Removal rule: if the cover needs more than one main claim plus one emphasis, remove text until one thought remains.

## Composition Rules
- Use shallow-depth studio portrait or cinematic talking-head frame.
- Keep the background plain, blurred, gray/black/white, or a subdued UI layer.
- Favor close-up or chest-up portrait; eyes must be clear.
- Use asymmetry: text mass on one side, face on the other.
- Keep the face larger than any background object.
- Use hands only if they communicate thinking, warning, or explanation. Avoid chaotic arm poses.

## Subject Rules
- Subject is a calm authority figure, not an entertainer.
- Expression: serious, focused, slightly confrontational, or quietly intense.
- Pose: direct stare, slight lean-in, hand near face/temple, hands together, or one restrained explaining gesture.
- Clothing: simple black/gray hoodie or T-shirt; no flashy styling.
- Reference-image rule: preserve identity, not pose. Redesign pose to match the claim unless the user explicitly locks the original pose.

## Text Rules
- Use large bold geometric or grotesk sans-serif.
- White type on dark background or black type on white plate.
- Use lowercase for conversational thesis covers; use all caps only for blunt commands or market warnings.
- Use one highlight device at most: white plate, pale yellow word, or red underline.
- Use no outline/glow-heavy gaming style.
- Do not overcrowd the thumbnail with more than one idea.

## Typography Layout System
- Text hierarchy: main claim dominates. Emphasis word is second-level but still inside the same sentence.
- Protected text zones: left-side clean background band for 16:9; center-safe stacked text for vertical adaptations.
- Backing system: white rectangular highlight plate behind the hinge phrase; no rounded pill clutter.
- Background isolation: darken/blur the background behind text; if background is light, use black text or a white plate with clear contrast.
- Clutter handling: remove background detail before reducing text size. Dan-style thumbnails need confidence, not dense explanation.
- Paired-label behavior: avoid paired labels. If forced, keep them visually subordinate to the main claim.
- Platform crop safety: keep text and face inside the central 80% for Bilibili and Douyin crops; do not rely on edge text.

## Color And Lighting Rules
- Dominant palette: grayscale, charcoal, black, white, cool gray.
- Accent colors: pale yellow or red only for one word/underline.
- Saturation: low.
- Contrast: high between text and background; moderate cinematic contrast on face.
- Lighting: soft key light on face, dark vignette, blurred room or UI background.

## Hook Mechanics
- Contrarian diagnosis: "the wrong skills", "social media is easy".
- Identity reframe: "the future belongs to generalists".
- Transformation promise: "your life will look drastically different".
- Command: "write like this", "steal these".
- Paradox: "you need to be bored".
- Market shift warning: "the death of info products".

## GPT Image 2 Prompt Rules
- Start with the approved claim and the viewer pain it resolves.
- Specify the frame as a serious creator/educator YouTube cover, not a colorful content-creator thumbnail.
- Define face placement, expression, and hand action.
- Define a protected text zone and exact typographic hierarchy.
- Keep background simple and blurred; use UI/proof object only when the topic is software, workflow, social media, or prompts.
- Include exact canvas and aspect ratio.
- Include negative constraints against spectacle, collage, neon, many icons, and misread text adjacency.

## Avoid
- MrBeast-style stakes, money, shock, giant props, arrows everywhere, and exaggerated facial emotion.
- Generic AI imagery: glowing robots, cyber grids, holograms, infinite circuit backgrounds.
- Product-ad layout where the app screenshot is the hero and the person becomes a small presenter.
- Multi-label comparison boards.
- Tiny text, weak contrast, or text placed on busy backgrounds.
- Copying Dan Koe's exact face, brand marks, or personal likeness. Use the user's own portrait if a human subject is needed.
