# PigeonYang Cover Design Standard: MrBeast

## Scope

This standard converts the public MrBeast thumbnail strategy into reusable, original cover rules for PigeonYang workflows.

Use it for:

- high-stakes video covers
- comparison videos
- survival/endurance challenges
- group competition videos
- prize/reward fantasy covers
- GPT Image 2 prompt writing

Do not use it for:

- exact recreation of existing MrBeast thumbnails
- use of MrBeast's face, logo, clothing, or protected recurring marks
- Squid Game or other IP-specific visual references unless the user owns rights
- quiet editorial covers where high-click spectacle would misrepresent the content

## Core Design DNA

1. One-glance premise: the viewer should understand the video promise in under one second.
2. Oversized human focal point: use a large expressive host or subject as the visual anchor.
3. Extreme contrast pair: cheap vs expensive, safe vs dangerous, poor vs luxury, team A vs team B, or early state vs late state.
4. Physical stakes: show money, day count, danger, prize, crowd size, or rule boundary as visible objects.
5. Minimal text: use zero to four words or one numeric label, not a sentence.
6. High-saturation commercial realism: crisp composite lighting, saturated blue/red/gold/white, high contrast, simplified background.
7. Title-cover unity: the cover should complete the title, not repeat it.
8. Hook-driven host action: the subject's pose and expression must express the current click promise; do not inherit a default reference pose unless it supports the hook.

## Cover Generation Engine

- Engine type: `Stakes Engine`.
- Viewer decision compressed: "Is this extreme enough, valuable enough, dangerous enough, or absurd enough that I need to see the outcome?"
- Topic-to-cover mechanism: convert the raw topic into a visible extreme event with a clear win/loss, cost/reward, before/after, survival pressure, quantity gap, prize, or physical rule.
- Subject role: the host is not a model or narrator; the host is a participant, presenter, referee, challenger, or witness inside the event.
- Pre-visual decision: before choosing colors, layout, or text, identify the one visible stake: money, time, quantity, danger, reward, failure, crowd, or scale.
- Drift risk: if an abstract topic becomes a workflow diagram, course poster, software dashboard, or calm expert portrait, it has left the MrBeast engine even if the colors and text look loud.

## Topic Translation Rules

- Abstract topics: must become physical stakes or a visible event. Turn systems into giant machines, loops into physical devices, efficiency into extreme output, knowledge into a challenge, and improvement into a before/after result.
- Concrete topics: exaggerate the scale, reward, danger, cost, time, or win/loss so the premise reads without explanation.
- Required translation variables: who is under pressure, what is at stake, what changes from left to right, what visible object proves the result, and what one number or short label can express the scale.
- Forbidden translation moves: do not render abstract topics as pure flowcharts, infographics, lecture slides, SaaS dashboards, calm productivity posters, or generic AI-tech compositions.
- Example translation: "AI content flywheel" should not become a clean circular diagram. It should become a giant AI content machine that automatically spits out videos, views, followers, or cash while the no-flywheel side shows one creator overwhelmed by tasks, failed uploads, and broken workflow debris.

## Layout Patterns

### Pattern 1: Price Ladder

- Use when: the video compares cheap vs expensive, basic vs premium, tiny vs massive, failure vs luxury.
- Composition: left side is visibly cheap, dirty, broken, or tiny; center has the host face; right side is huge, bright, luxurious, or impossible.
- Text: use labels such as `$1`, `$1M`, `$100M`, `$1B`; do not write the full title.
- Color: left side gray/dirty/stormy, right side blue/gold/clean.
- Avoid: showing too many price steps; the cover should show the two extremes.

### Pattern 2: Survival / Endurance

- Use when: the video involves surviving, waiting, being trapped, completing days, or resisting a challenge.
- Composition: one dramatic environment, host visibly strained, one clear rule marker such as a circle, raft, bunker, storm, timer, or day label.
- Text: use `DAY 6`, `DAY 97`, `DAY 99`, or one short progress marker.
- Color: danger colors and environmental contrast; the host must remain readable.
- Avoid: generic dark cinematic scenes where the rule is unclear.

### Pattern 3: Group Competition

- Use when: the video has teams, crowds, age brackets, social conflict, contestants, or elimination.
- Composition: rows, crowds, or opposing teams create scale; host can be foreground left or center.
- Text: often no text if uniforms, crowd geometry, or team colors communicate the conflict.
- Color: red vs blue or two clean team palettes.
- Avoid: crowds that become noisy texture at mobile size.

### Pattern 4: Prize Fantasy

- Use when: the video centers on winning, buying, giving away, or exploring an extraordinary object/place.
- Composition: large foreground host plus a dream prize environment or oversized prize object.
- Text: optional; use one label or arrow only if the prize value is unclear.
- Color: tropical cyan, clean white, gold, saturated green, bright sky.
- Avoid: subtle luxury; the prize must be instantly desirable.

## Subject Rules

- Use the user's own portrait only if the user supplies a reference image.
- If no portrait is supplied, use a generic original host or make the object/prize the main subject.
- The host should react to the premise: excited for reward, strained for survival, shocked for danger, confident for comparison.
- The host pose must be selected from the layout pattern, not copied from a supplied character sheet or portrait by default.
- Show stakes as physical objects: cash, timer, prize, boundary, crowd, wreckage, luxury object.
- Do not use MrBeast's likeness.

## Reference Image Handling

- Use user-supplied portrait or character references to preserve identity traits only: face structure, hair, glasses, clothing, silhouette, color palette, and recognizable accessories.
- Do not copy the reference image's pose, hand gesture, facial expression, camera angle, sheet layout, or static mood unless the user explicitly requests it.
- If the reference shows a calm or thinking pose, override it with a hook-driven thumbnail action.
- For price ladder or before/after contrast: make the host point toward the winning side, push away the losing side, or physically separate the two sides.
- For survival/endurance: make the host visibly strained, leaning forward, crawling, bracing, gripping the rule boundary, or reacting to danger.
- For group competition: make the host act like an energetic presenter, referee, or instigator, pointing toward the conflict.
- For prize fantasy: make the host open arms, point, lean toward the prize, or show surprised excitement.
- Prompt wording must include both:
  - `Preserve identity traits from the reference image: ...`
  - `Do not copy the reference pose; use this new hook-driven pose: ...`

## Text Rules

- Prefer 0-4 words.
- Use huge bold sans-serif uppercase text.
- Use white fill with heavy black stroke when text is on a busy background.
- Place text in a clean corner or safe band.
- Use numeric labels before explanatory words.
- Do not use small subtitle-like text.
- Do not put the full video title on the cover.

## Typography Layout System

- Treat text as a separate protected design layer, not decoration floating over the image.
- Define a three-level hierarchy before prompting:
  - Main title: the largest phrase, placed in a top-center or center-right safe zone.
  - State labels: smaller paired labels for the left/right states, placed in matched badges.
  - Optional numeric or progress badge: one short marker such as `$1`, `$1M`, `DAY 99`, or an arrow label.
- Every text block must have one of these protections:
  - clean negative space behind it
  - solid or semi-transparent backing plate
  - colored badge/sticker shape
  - thick black stroke plus shadow on a simple background
- For before/after or price-ladder thumbnails:
  - Use matched left/right badges with the same size, padding, font weight, and stroke.
  - Put the left label in an upper-left or left-center safe area, not on top of messy UI.
  - Put the right label in an upper-right or right-center safe area, not on top of the flywheel/prize details.
  - Use semantic badge colors: gray/red/dark for the losing or chaotic side, blue/gold/green for the winning or systemized side.
- For Bilibili 16:9:
  - Keep the main title, paired labels, face, and hook object inside the center-safe area.
  - Avoid placing text at extreme left/right edges because card crops can damage it.
- For 3:4 or 9:16:
  - Stack text into top, middle, and lower safe bands.
  - Do not use wide left/right labels unless they remain inside the vertical safe area.
- Use Chinese display typography deliberately:
  - thick Chinese variety-show thumbnail lettering
  - high contrast fill and stroke
  - consistent style across all labels
  - no random English or extra filler text
- If the background is intentionally chaotic, create a clean text plate first, then place the text.
- Text must not overlap the host face, hands, flywheel center, product, or dense UI details.

## Color And Lighting Rules

- Use saturated blues, bright reds, clean whites, and gold/yellow accents.
- Push contrast between bad and good sides.
- Keep faces bright, sharp, and readable.
- Use crisp composite realism rather than painterly illustration.
- Simplify background clutter.

## Hook Mechanics

- Price ladder: "from worthless to absurdly valuable."
- Endurance countdown: "almost failed, almost won."
- Group competition: "many people, one clear conflict."
- Prize fantasy: "this impossible thing could be won or experienced."
- Danger: "the rule or environment threatens the subject."

## GPT Image 2 Prompt Contract

Every prompt must specify:

- final artifact: finished video cover/thumbnail
- platform and ratio
- platform adaptation decision
- cover generation engine and topic translation decision
- topic and click promise
- layout pattern
- main subject and reference image handling
- hook-driven host pose/action
- exact on-image text or "no text"
- typography hierarchy and protected text zones
- composition hierarchy
- color/lighting rules
- mobile readability constraints
- negative constraints

Do not prompt "in MrBeast's exact style." Prompt the transferable rules instead.

## Negative Constraints

- no MrBeast likeness
- no copied existing thumbnail
- no copied logos, watermarks, or protected IP
- no extra text
- no misspelled text
- no unreadable small text
- no text placed directly on cluttered backgrounds without a backing shape
- no inconsistent text styles between matched labels
- no cluttered collage
- no generic poster that hides the video promise
- no edge-critical details outside platform safe areas
- no unintentional copying of a reference pose, hand gesture, or character-sheet layout

## User Intake Questions

Ask in this order:

1. Which platform will this cover be published on?
2. Which mode or orientation is needed: Bilibili native, Bilibili cross-platform, 4:3-safe, horizontal, vertical, square, long video, or short video?
3. What is the video topic or working title?
4. What exact on-cover text should appear? If none, say no text.
5. Which pattern fits best: price ladder, survival/endurance, group competition, prize fantasy, or other?
6. Should the user's own face appear? If yes, request a reference image.
7. Should the reference pose be preserved, or should the pose be redesigned for the hook? Default: redesign for the hook.
8. What must appear?
9. What must not appear?
10. Are there brand, legal, or platform constraints?

## Quality Checklist

- Can the premise be understood in one second?
- Does the selected ratio match the platform?
- Are face, text, and hook object inside the platform safe area?
- Is there one dominant focal hierarchy?
- Is the on-image text short and readable at phone size?
- Does every text block have a protected readable zone, badge, or clean background?
- Are paired labels visually matched and semantically colored?
- Does the cover promise match the actual video topic?
- Did the output avoid copying MrBeast's identity or a specific existing thumbnail?
- Did the host action communicate the hook, instead of passively copying the reference image pose?
